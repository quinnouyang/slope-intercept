Slope-intercept Formula: \(y=mx+b\)

The classic slope-intercept formula: it defines itself! There are two constants that you can "control": the slope \(m\) and the \(y\)-intercept \((0, b)\).

The \(x\) and \(y\) variables are numbers "bound" by these constants. It's easier to interpret \(x\) as an "input" and \(y\) as an "output," which is why you sometimes see this equation as a function: \(f(x) = mx + b\).

Take any number and set \(x\) equal to it in the formula. As you've already defined the slope and y-intercept, you can calculate the remaining variable: \(y\).

The slope is traditionally called the "rate of change" or described as "rise over run." It indicates how much the \(y\)-value changes for every unit increase in the \(x\)-value. The y-intercept is the \(y\)-value that you get from plugging in \(x=0\) and where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis (where \(x=0\)).

Graphing this equation on a coordinate plane shows a linear line that maps all the possible \(x\)-values to the \(y\)-values. Try plugging in \(x\)-values yourself and notice how the \(y\)-values match what the formula gives!
